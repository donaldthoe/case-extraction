Case 1

Kesatuan Eksekutif RHB Bank Berhad v RHB Bank Berhad

No. Kes 4/2-2565/18
Award Award No 233 of 2023
Presiding YA Tuan Augustine Anthony
Pages 12-51
Collective Agreement

Summary

Kesatuan Eksekutif RHB Bank Berhad (the Union) brought a trade dispute against RHB Bank Berhad (the Bank) concerning the terms and conditions to be incorporated into the parties' 1st Collective Agreement for the period 1 January 2014 to 31 December 2016. The dispute was referred to the Industrial Court by the Minister of Human Resources on 3 August 2018 pursuant to Section 26(2) of the Industrial Relations Act 1967. The Union proposed 42 articles while the Bank counter-proposed 41; the parties agreed on 24 articles, leaving 17 articles in dispute covering matters such as parties, effective date, check-off, transfer, misconduct, rates of pay, annual increment, allowances, hours of work, annual leave, medical benefits, special leave, retrenchment, new system and technology, exclusion, implementation, and payment of arrears. The Court determined each disputed article, adopting a mix of the Union's and Bank's proposals based on equity, financial implications, and industry practice under Section 30(4) of the Act. Notable findings included: the effective date was fixed at 1 January 2020; the Union's proposal on retrenchment consultation was rejected as falling within managerial prerogative under Section 13(3)(d); and the Union's proposal on video display unit health benefits was accepted as fair and consistent with industry standards. The Court ordered that all agreed articles and the Court's determinations on disputed articles be incorporated into and form the 1st Collective Agreement, constituting the Award.

Persatuan Bank-Bank Perdagangan Tanah Melayu v National Union of Bank Employees, States of Malaya

No. Kes 42/478/24
Award Award No 1679 of 2024
Presiding YA Tuan Paramalingam J. Doraisamy
Pages 52-58
Trade Dispute

Summary

The National Union of Bank Employees (the Union) applied for the issuance of a subpoena (Form O) against the Minister of Human Resources, Y.B. Steven Sim Chee Keong, to compel his testimony in a trade dispute between Persatuan Bank-Bank Perdagangan Tanah Melayu (MCBA) and the Union concerning the validity of a Memorandum of Agreement on Festival Aid dated 5 April 2023. MCBA objected, arguing the Minister was not a party to the MOA, was not even the Minister when it was executed, and that calling him would be an abuse of process. The Union contended the Minister had secretly met with MCBA regarding Festival Aid payments for 2024 without the Union's knowledge, and his evidence was relevant. The Court found that the trade dispute was squarely about the validity of the MOA dated 5 April 2023, to which the Minister had no involvement whatsoever. The Union failed to produce any evidence of the Minister's alleged participation in a meeting with MCBA. Applying the principles from Wong Sin Chong v Bhagwan Singh and Ismail v Hasnul on the proper exercise of subpoena powers and abuse of process, the Court concluded the Union's application was a fishing expedition and an abuse of the Court's process. The Court allowed MCBA's objection and refused the issuance of the subpoena against the Minister.

Case 3

Persatuan Bank-Bank Perdagangan Tanah Melayu v National Union of Bank Employees, States of Malaya

No. Kes 22/3-478/24
Award Award No 174 of 2025
Presiding YA Tuan Paramalingam J. Doraisamy
Pages 59-76
Trade Dispute

Summary

Persatuan Bank-Bank Perdagangan Tanah Melayu (MCBA) and the National Union of Bank Employees (the Union) were in dispute over the validity and effect of a Memorandum of Agreement on Festival Aid dated 5 April 2023, under which member banks of MCBA paid one month's salary as Festival Aid to Union members for the year 2023. The Union contended the MOA obligated MCBA to continue paying Festival Aid for 2024 and beyond, while MCBA argued the payment was a one-off arrangement for 2023 only and that the MOA had no legal or binding effect as it was never deposited with the Industrial Court for cognizance. The Court found that the MOA constituted a collective agreement within the meaning of Section 2 of the Industrial Relations Act 1967, and that under Sections 16(1) and 17(2) of the Act, as well as Articles 3(1) and 3(3) of the 19th Collective Agreement, it was mandatory for the MOA to be deposited for cognizance in order to have legal effect. The Court relied on established precedent including Non-Metallic Mineral Products Manufacturing Employees' Union v South East Fire Bricks, the NUBE v HSBC Bank Malaysia Berhad line of cases affirmed up to the Federal Court, and found the MOA was clearly intended as a one-off payment for 2023 only. The Court unanimously held that the MOA had no legal or binding effect and dismissed the Union's claim for Festival Aid payments post-2023.

National Union of Bank Employees v HSBC Bank Malaysia Berhad

No. Kes 19/3-1343/22
Award Award No 206 of 2025
Presiding YA Puan Hajah Zalina binti Haji Awang@Mamat
Pages 77-86
Trade Dispute

Summary

The National Union of Bank Employees (NUBE) brought a trade dispute against HSBC Bank Malaysia Berhad (the Bank) alleging non-compliance with the grievance procedure under Article 4 of the MCBA/NUBE Collective Agreement. The dispute arose from a grievance lodged by Sivananthan Jegatheeswaran (UW-1), an IRC representative and Bank employee, on 25 November 2019 concerning perceived lack of transparency by the Bank regarding a Voluntary Separation Scheme (VSS) exercise. The grievance progressed through the Office Level Meeting on 17 December 2019 and the Branch Level Meeting on 6 February 2020, but remained unresolved. The Union attempted to escalate the matter to a National Level Meeting, but disputes arose over venue and conduct, and no proper written notice was served as required by Article 4(2)(d)(i). The Bank argued that NUBE itself breached the prescribed timelines under Article 4, including the seven-day filing requirement, and failed to properly escalate the grievance through the contractual procedure. The Court found that NUBE and UW-1 had failed to comply with the mandatory timelines and procedural steps prescribed under Article 4 of the Collective Agreement, relying on the Court of Appeal's affirmation in Malayan Banking Berhad v Kesatuan Kebangsaan Pekerja-Pekerja Bank that failure to file a grievance within seven days was fatal. The Court further noted that UW-1 had not taken the VSS and was not otherwise affected by it, rendering the grievance academic. The Court unanimously dismissed the Union's application.

David Chew Yu Lin v BFM Media Sdn Bhd

No. Kes 21(31)(21)/4-726/19
Award Award No 470 of 2024
Presiding YA Tuan Syed Noh Bin Said Nazir @ Syed Nadzir
Pages 87-110
Salah Laku / Misconduct — Sexual Harassment

Summary

David Chew Yu Lin was employed by BFM Media Sdn Bhd as a Presenter/Producer from 1 January 2010, with a last drawn salary of RM10,100.00 per month. Following an anonymous email circulated in November 2018 alleging sexual harassment at the company, BFM appointed an Independent Investigating Committee comprising external lawyers to investigate. The Claimant was interviewed on 14 December 2018 and subsequently issued a Notice to Show Cause on 24 December 2018 containing allegations that he had sexually harassed an intern, Angeline Teh Ying Han, by placing his hand on her lap in a car and insisting she accept an unsolicited iPad purchase, and that he had kissed another female employee without consent in a car park. The Claimant was dismissed on 4 January 2019. The Industrial Court found that the Company proved the sexual harassment charges on a balance of probabilities. The Court held that the Claimant offered only bare denials and gave evasive and inconsistent testimony, while the complainants were credible witnesses with no motive to fabricate allegations. The Court applied the two-fold test for sexual harassment and found the touching was sexual in nature and unwelcome. The delay in reporting by the complainants was held to be immaterial. The Court concluded that the dismissal was with just cause or excuse and that the punishment of dismissal was proportionate given the employer's duty to provide a safe working environment. The Claimant's case was dismissed. No compensation was awarded.

Award Award No 191 of 2024
Presiding YA Tuan Andersen Ong Wai Leong
Pages 111-119
Salah Laku / Misconduct — Assault of Subordinate

Summary

Paul Benjimen a/l Silva was employed by PWB (M) Sdn Bhd, a facilities management company, as an Operation Executive I. He had joined the Company on 15 November 2014 as a Site Supervisor and was subsequently promoted. The Company charged the Claimant with assaulting his subordinate, Rahman Mohammad Mahbubur, a Bangladeshi cleaner, on two separate occasions at Tesco Mall in Puchong and IOI City Mall in Putrajaya. A domestic inquiry was held on 17 August 2020 and the panel unanimously found the Claimant guilty. His employment was terminated effective 24 August 2020. Physical violence against employees, including foreign workers, must never be tolerated in the workplace. The Court affirmed that the punishment of dismissal was appropriate and proportionate, following the precedent in Kian Joo Can Factory Berhad v Ng Kok Hooi. The Claimant's case was dismissed. Presiding YA Tuan Mohd Taufik bin Mohd@Mohd Yusoff
Pages 120-136
Salah Laku / Misconduct — Use of Vulgar Words

Summary

M Anathan a/l Muniandy joined HS Optimus Wood Products Sdn Bhd (formerly KLW Wood Products) on 4 April 2005 as a Production Coordinator and was promoted over the years to General Manager, with a last drawn salary of RM15,750.00 per month. On 30 April 2019, the Claimant sent WhatsApp messages in a company group using vulgar words and showing disrespect towards the Chief Marketing Officer, Mr Albert Tan Sai Beng. He was also charged with yelling and screaming at Director Mr Chia Fook Sam during a telephone conversation, and with giving false representation to a client (SIAC) regarding project completion dates. On the third charge, the Claimant's email correspondence confirmed he had given his director the impression everything was proceeding as planned when it was not. The Court held the dismissal was proportionate given the Claimant's senior managerial position, the seriousness of the misconduct, and the irreparable breach of trust with two company directors. The Claimant's case was dismissed. No compensation was awarded.

Muhammad bin Abdullah, a Mechanical Technician employed by ExxonMobil Exploration and Production Malaysia Inc since April 2014, was assigned to the Company's offshore Lawit-A platform in Terengganu. Yeoh Chin Hock commenced employment with Ikat Tegas Jaya Sdn Bhd on 1 September 2021 as Senior Project Manager with a monthly salary of RM15,000, engaged on a fixed-term basis for a three-year construction project in Port Dickson. The Company denied ever employing the Claimant, asserting no letter of appointment was issued. However, during cross-examination, the Company's Managing Director, David Chong, admitted that his business partner had instructed him to offer the Claimant the position, that the Company had paid the Claimant's September 2021 salary as evidenced by a bank statement and payment voucher, and that the Claimant had worked for the Company for one month and 22 days. The Court found David Chong to be an unreliable witness who had lied under oath on multiple points in his witness statement. The Court determined that an employment relationship existed notwithstanding the absence of a formal written contract, that the Claimant was summarily dismissed on 22 October 2021 without just cause or excuse, and that the Company provided no bona fide reasons for the dismissal. The Court awarded the Claimant 24 months of backwages at RM15,000 per month totalling RM360,000, less a 30% deduction for post-dismissal earnings, plus RM15,000 for unpaid October 2021 salary, resulting in a total award of RM267,000.

Award Interim / Interim Award — Strike Off — Statutory Body

Summary

Sugumaran a/l Nagappan was employed by Pembangunan Sumber Manusia Berhad as Vice President (Grade M) under successive fixed-term contracts commencing 13 July 2020. His second contract was subsequently revised via an addendum reducing its duration, and his services ceased on 31 December 2021 upon contract expiry. The Company applied to strike out the Claimant's case on the ground that it is a statutory authority within the meaning of Section 2 of the Industrial Relations Act 1967, and that Section 52 of the Act excludes employees of statutory authorities from the protections of Part VI, which includes the remedy of reinstatement under Section 20. The Court found that the Company, established under the Pembangunan Sumber Manusia Act 2001, operating under full ministerial control with a board appointed by the Minister of Human Resources, is indeed a statutory authority. The Court further held that under Section 52(3), Part VI applies to employees of a statutory authority only if the Minister has issued a Gazette order prescribing the name of that authority, and no such order had been made for the Company. Following the Court of Appeal precedent in Institut Integriti Malaysia v Roziah Binti Harun, the Court allowed the Company's application and struck out the Claimant's case for want of jurisdiction.

Non-Compliance

Summary

Kesatuan Kakitangan Ipoh Cargo Terminal Sdn Bhd filed a non-compliance complaint under Section 56(1) of the Industrial Relations Act 1967 against Ipoh Cargo Terminal Sdn Bhd for failing to pay annual salary increments under Article 20 of the 7th Collective Agreement (Award No. 3167 of 2018) for the years 2021 and 2022. The Company contended that the 7th CA expired on 31 December 2020 and that the annual increment was discretionary, further citing financial losses due to COVID-19 as justification for non-payment. The Court, applying a broad and liberal approach to interpretation as mandated by the Court of Appeal in Kesatuan Pekerja-Pekerja Perkayuan v Syarikat Jengka Sdn Bhd, found that Articles 3 and 11.3 of the 7th CA clearly provided for continuity of terms until superseded by a new collective agreement. Since no new agreement had been concluded, the 7th CA remained in force. The Court also rejected the Company's preliminary objection of res judicata and held that financial incapacity per se does not constitute special circumstances under Section 56(2)(c). The Court unanimously ordered the Company to comply with Article 20 forthwith but dismissed the Union's application for 8% interest and costs.

Summary

Financial incapacity was held not to constitute special circumstances under Section 56(2)(c). The Restraining Order was found inapplicable as it named only KNM Group Berhad and KNM Process System Sdn Bhd, not the Respondent Company. The Court ordered the Respondent Company to pay RM105,000 forthwith.
